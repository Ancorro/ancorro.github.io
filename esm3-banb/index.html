Evaluating Frozen and Antibody-Adapted ESM3 Representations for HIV Broadly Neutralizing Antibody Modeling

Steven Cleasby-Mayeda Oregon State University cleasbys@oregonstate.edu steven.cleasby.mayeda@gmail.com
AI539 — Scientific Foundation Models
June 2026

Abstract

We evaluate whether representations from the ESM3 protein foundation model transfer to sequence-only modeling of HIV broadly neutralizing antibodies (bnAbs), and whether domain adaptation via parameter-efficient fine-tuning alters that transfer. We partition heavy-chain sequences into lineage groups and evaluate frozen ESM3 and domain-adapted representations on classification and regression tasks. Frozen ESM3 representations effectively predict bnAb neutralization breadth and classify broad versus narrow neutralizers, outperforming handcrafted baseline features. Unsupervised domain adaptation on antibody repertoires does not yield significant downstream improvements. Representation interpolation along lineage maturation pathways shows that raw embedding-space interpolation is comparable to specialized variational autoencoder paths. These findings support frozen ESM3 as a viable exploratory representation for antibody modeling, while highlighting the importance of robust validation controls.

1. Introduction

HIV-1 broadly neutralizing antibodies (bnAbs) neutralize diverse viral variants by binding conserved envelope glycoprotein regions [1][2]. Unlike ordinary antibodies, bnAbs mature over years through somatic hypermutation, producing clonal lineages that share an unmutated common ancestor (UCA) but diverge in sequence and function (Figure 1).

Predicting neutralization breadth from variable heavy-chain ($V_H$) sequence alone is difficult because functional readouts depend on heterogeneous virus panels and inconsistent laboratory assays.

Protein language models learn contextual sequence representations from large unlabeled corpora. ESM3 reasons jointly over sequence, structure, and function [3][4]. We ask whether general-protein ESM3 embeddings support out-of-lineage bnAb modeling from heavy-chain sequence alone, and whether unsupervised adaptation on the Observed Antibody Space (OAS) [5] improves transfer.

Because antibody datasets contain somatically related variants, naive random splits leak lineage information across folds. We implement metadata-derived lineage grouping with group-resampled uncertainty analysis on CATNAP [6], then compare handcrafted features, frozen ESM3, and OAS-tuned ESM3 (LoRA) under identical folds and heads.

2. Related Work

CATNAP standardizes antibody–virus neutralization panels and metadata [6]. Longitudinal lineage studies show breadth can change substantially as somatic variants accumulate [7][8], motivating lineage-held-out evaluation.

Most computational HIV neutralization work predicts virus resistance to a fixed antibody panel rather than antibody breadth from sequence [9]. Recent methods such as BRAVE couple ESM2 embeddings of antibody and virus sequences to predict resistance across many bnAbs [10]. We evaluate the converse: antibody-centric breadth estimation from heavy-chain sequence.

ESM2 demonstrated evolutionary-scale representation learning [11]; ESM3 extends multimodal reasoning [3]. Antibody-specific models (AntiBERTy, AbLang, IgBERT) train on immunoglobulin repertoires [12][13][14], yet surveys find general protein LMs can match domain models when size and layer selection are controlled [15].

We adapt ESM3 with LoRA [16] on OAS via masked language modeling, then compare frozen versus adapted embeddings. We also test whether VAE latent interpolation [17] outperforms straight-line interpolation in raw ESM3 embedding space along maturation trajectories.

3. Data and Evaluation Protocol

From CATNAP we compile $V_H$ sequences with quantitative neutralization labels. Breadth for antibody $i$ is the fraction of tested antibody–virus pairs neutralized below $\text{IC}_{50} \leq 50\,\mu\mathrm{g/mL}$:

$$b_i = \frac{\#\{\text{measurements with IC}_{50}\leq 50\,\mu\mathrm{g/mL}\}}{\#\{\text{tested antibody--virus measurements}\}}$$

We require at least 20 tested strains per antibody. Binary labels assign broad ($1$) when $b_i \geq 0.50$ and narrow ($0$) when $b_i \leq 0.20$, excluding the intermediate band. The labeled cohort contains 300 unique $V_H$ sequences (202 broad, 98 narrow), length 116–146 residues (Figure 2).

Lineage groups combine CATNAP clonal metadata (216 antibodies), shared-donor metadata (70), single-linkage clustering at $\geq 80\%$ Levenshtein identity (2), and singletons (12), yielding 113 group IDs. Both tasks use one 5-fold StratifiedGroupKFold split so entire lineages remain in train or validation.

Classification metrics: AUROC and AUPRC. Regression metrics: Pearson $r$, Spearman $\rho$, and $R^2$. Configuration selection uses mean fold score; reporting uses pooled out-of-fold (OOF) predictions with 95% intervals from 10,000 lineage-group bootstrap resamples.

OAS adaptation uses 219,837 deduplicated human heavy-chain sequences (108,675 clonotypes) from 11 data units, with clonotype-held-out train/validation splits. OAS supplies adaptation data only—it is not labeled for bnAb breadth and does not enter downstream test folds.

4. Representations and Models

4.1. Handcrafted baseline

Seven features per sequence: length; charged, hydrophobic, aromatic, and cysteine fractions; cysteine count; and $N$-glycosylation-motif count. XGBoost [18] is the selected baseline head.

4.2. Frozen ESM3

Residue embeddings from frozen esm3-sm-open-v1 (1.4B parameters) [4] yield $L \times 1536$ matrices per sequence. We compare mean pooling (1536-d) and concatenated mean/max/std pooling (4608-d). Scaling and PCA fit strictly inside each training fold.

4.3. OAS LoRA adaptation

Rank-16 LoRA ($\alpha=32$, dropout 0.05) trains 18.9M parameters (1.33%) on masked language modeling [19] for one epoch (AdamW [20]). Clonotype-held-out validation reaches loss $0.5025$ (perplexity $1.653$). Adapters merge into the base model before embedding extraction.

4.4. Per-residue VAE

A VAE compresses 1536-d residue embeddings to 32-d latents (hidden 512/256, $\beta=0.005$ KL warmup). Endpoints align with Needleman–Wunsch [21] before interpolating VAE latents, reconstructed straight lines, and raw ESM3 straight lines along UCA-to-mature paths.

5. Results

5.1. Classification

Frozen ESM3 improves pooled AUROC from $0.625$ (handcrafted XGB) to $0.746$ and pooled AUPRC from $0.742$ to $0.859$ (Table 1). Paired frozen-minus-handcrafted difference is $+0.121$ AUROC ($[0.012, 0.228]$). LoRA reaches pooled AUROC $0.726$, below frozen by $-0.020$ ($[-0.096, 0.059]$)—not establishing downstream benefit from the single adaptation run.

5.2. Continuous breadth regression

Frozen ESM3 reaches pooled Spearman $\rho = 0.366$ ($[0.126, 0.585]$), Pearson $r = 0.357$, and $R^2 = 0.114$; the length-only Spearman floor is $0.004$ (Table 2). LoRA-selected random forest with mean pooling yields pooled $\rho = 0.319$; paired LoRA-minus-frozen difference is $-0.047$ ($[-0.169, 0.064]$).

5.3. VAE interpolation

For seven VRC34 inferred intermediates, raw ESM3 interpolation is closer than the VAE path for six cases (raw MAE mean $20.77$ vs VAE $37.47$). Midpoint tests on four ordered triples show raw-line MAE $8.24$–$13.35$ versus VAE midpoint $35.20$–$37.95$. LoRA VAE maturation correlations rise descriptively (mean Pearson $0.566 \rightarrow 0.822$) but lack raw-embedding controls and cover only four lineages (Figure 5).

6. Limitations

Heads and pooling were selected on the same grouped folds used for reporting, so cross-validation scores are optimistic; nested grouped CV or locked external holdouts are needed for confirmatory claims.

The cohort is small (300 sequences, 113 groups). Only $V_H$ chains are modeled—light-chain pairing and structure are omitted. Grouping heuristics cannot guarantee complete developmental independence.

LoRA adaptation was a single run with one configuration. The per-residue VAE ignores sequence order and tertiary contacts and failed round-trip sequence decoding, so interpolation claims remain speculative.

7. Conclusion

Under lineage-grouped evaluation, frozen ESM3 improves over handcrafted sequence features for CATNAP bnAb classification (pooled AUROC $0.746$ vs $0.625$) and shows modest breadth regression signal ($\rho = 0.366$), though absolute prediction remains weak. Unsupervised OAS LoRA did not improve downstream performance in this run.

Prior HIV ML work is predominantly virus-centric; we instead classify breadth directly from heavy-chain sequence under epitope-agnostic, lineage-held-out splits. Raw embedding interpolation matched or beat VAE paths on VRC34 intermediates. The strongest contribution is methodological: lineage-aware splits, pooled-versus-fold reporting, and group-resampled uncertainty change how these experiments should be interpreted.
